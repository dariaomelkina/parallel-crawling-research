“You told me once to forget the past, cause it doesn’t matter, but you’re the one still tied to the past, Spike.”-- Faye Valentine

This one dialogue perfectly encapsulates everything that Cowboy Bebop is. It’s a struggle between trying to break the shackles of the past yet the more you walk forward the more you realize that you are just falling deeper into the pit of your own past because despite your better judgement, you are what your past defines you to be. Sometimes we can break this paradox and free ourselves from the past but most of us are just stuck on this endless loop of the regrets of our earlier days.

Cowboy Bebop is set in a futuristic western setting that is clearly inspired by Western pop-culture. This is very evident from the homage that Bebop pays to the likes of Sergio Leone’s classic The Good, The Bad and The Ugly and the Bruce Lee inspired fight choreography in the very first episode. We follow the journey of two space bounty hunters, Spike and Jet. They try to catch criminals in space and earn reward for their hardwork. After awhile they get two more crews, Faye and Edward. Faye is a sexy yet problematic woman who is always on debt due to her rather unusual spending and Edward is a young computer genius. Their crew is also joined by Pembroke Welsh Corgi named Ein. Since Cowboy Bebop is an episodic show it predictably has a repeating formula. They do a mission, cause a lot of commotion and damage and once they go to collect their bounty they realize that the cost of the damage outweighs their bounty prize. So, basically they return to square one.

This is why I don’t like episodic shows yet Bebop despite it’s repeating formula stands out. This is because these repeated acts are not due to the writer’s inability to have creative assertion but they explore each character and their belief with great subtlety and care. A beautiful example of this is showcased on the very first episode. Both Spike and Jet witnesses a tragic end between two lovers but despite this, they both move on with the weight of this tragedy on their shoulder not holding them back. They have seen enough to know how cruel the world is and they know not to form any connection to people when everyone is trying to better one another. This is where the beautiful tragedy of Cowboy Bebop begins. Since this is a spoiler free review I won’t point out the specifics but watch Spike and Jet’s behavior towards the end of Episode 24. An ingenious reversal of how they were able to break free of that weight when they were not attached and how human attachment suddenly changes everything.

What truly separates Cowboy Bebop from every other show of this similar style is it’s characters. Each character is beautifully well designed with unique attributes to remember them. The back and forth between Bebop’s crew is one of the shows outer charm but what lies inside is the intrigue of their past and the decisions that made them who they are today. Unlike most stories, they don’t dump their story in one episode and be done with it. No, they carefully weave their characteristics with every episode. They gives us bits and pieces and even before their eventual reveal, we already know them enough to know how their past shaped them and how they shaped their past. By the end, everything beautifully falls into place as these characters chooses their path when they are standing at the crossroads of letting the sleep of your past drag you deeper into your dream or to wake up.

It’s mesmerizing to witness these characters finally reaching their own resolve with their broken past and chose their own redemption. Some make the right choice and some don’t but this is how life is.

Cowboy Bebop essentially has everything. It has the perfect start that sets up the preceding events beautifully, it has the perfect story midpoint in Jupiter Jazz Part 1&2 and it has one of the finest ending of the medium in The Real Folk Blues Part 1&2. On top of all that, it has what is highly regarded as one of the greatest episode in all of anime and what I consider to be my personal favorite episode in anime, Episode 5, Ballad of Fallen Angels. This episode introduces us to the show’s main antagonist, Vicious. It’s a perfect mixture of intrigue and high octane action that sets both Spike and Vicious on a path that they left unburied all those years ago. I have probably seen this episode atleast 50 times yet I can’t grow tired of watching it.

Cowboy Bebop doesn’t only let it’s narrative define it but it also lets it’s production define it as well. It is easily one of the most well directed tv show to be ever aired. Each episode is filled with magnificent scene composition that lets it’s perfect color shading and detailed background to flourish. One can not believe that this is actually the debut work of Shinichiro Watanabe who went on to make more great shows but none quite as definitive in it’s brilliance as Cowboy Bebop. It is often said that an artist’s first art is what defines them and Watanabe lets Cowboy Bebop’s sheer genius in story telling define and shape his legacy.

Now it can’t be a Cowboy Bebop review without talking about it’s brilliant musical score by the musical genius Yoko Kanno. The musical score of Cowboy bebop is just as important to it’s impressive legacy as it’s narrative brilliance. Yoko Kanno and her band Seatbelts created what is arguably the greatest musical score for a Television show. Each episode is blessed with fantastic tracks that compliments the amazing scene composition of Shinichiro Watanabe. The music mostly focuses on jazz but there is no lack of diversity throughout the soundtrack. The opening, Tank is one of the most recognizable and beloved anime opening ever. The ending track, The Real Folk Blues is a perfect mixture of melancholia and jazz that sets the mood of the show. Some stand out tracks includes Call Me Call Me, Elm, Space Lion. Every episode of Cowboy Bebop is named after either a famous song or a musical reference.

Now since this is an episodic anime, some episodes are bound to be better than the other ones and some will be weaker compared to the show’s peak. There are no outright bad episode in Cowboy Bebop and even it’s weaker episodes can easily claim to be better than most other anime’s peak points. The particular episodes I am not quite a big fan of are "Toys in the Attic" and "Boogie Woogie Feng Shui". They are by no means bad, it’s just that they are not as good as some of the other episodes.

Cowboy Bebop is a show which wears a mask. The mask is the fun action-packed show with cool dialogues with distinctive western style but what lies underneath the mask is the cruel truth of how we have to carry the weight of our past and move forward, not let that weight slow us down. You can try all you want to not be attached to your regrets but in the end it’s our past that defines us. Some people choose to walk away yet the others let their past sink them until their present reality becomes an extension of their past regrets.

I think we can all agree that Cowboy Bebop is a great series based on its tremendous amount of charisma, style, and flair alone. We all know its music, visual style, and setting is legendary. We all know Spike is the coolest character in all of anime and Faye is one of the sexiest, and yet realistically human femme fatales. We all know Ed is one of the most offbeat, unique, and fun characters.

In terms of substance, the show sparse and that's OK, because Cowboy Bebop proves that you don't need to tell some grand, complicated story in order to be considered good; it is perfectly acceptable to take simple but raw emotions and communicate them with such intensity and precision that the audience is absorbed into the world of the characters. I think a lot of people try to pull some existential meaning from something that they enjoy in order to validate their enjoyment of something, but I don't think that Cowboy Bebop was going for some higher meaning and there's nothing wrong with that. It does discuss the idea of dealing with one’s past, whether that means letting go of it and moving on or being shackled by it, but I personally didn’t find any of its ideas insightful.

Many of the negative reviews point to the show’s lack of attention to its overarching plot as a cause for lack of enjoyment and/or engagement. Once again, not everything has to tie into something bigger for greater significance. Episodic shows succeed by constantly introducing fresh characters and settings and combining these with interesting core characters to craft entertaining stories. There's more freedom to explore the world of the show as well as different sides of the characters this way. That being said, the short duration of the stories being told means that you really have to nail them by making each character, setting, moment, and new idea count or else the stories end up being forgettable. The 22 minute time frame limits the complexity of the stories you can tell, as you have to introduce new characters, new conflicts, and new settings and complete an arc for the characters and resolve said conflicts in the same episode. This leaves little room for error.

So did Cowboy Bebop manage to pull it off, telling memorable stories in each episode with moments so good you can watch them a thousand times over and not get bored? Sometimes, but not always. About half of the episodes fall into the category of "Fun, enjoyable little episode. Onto the next one." While each episode develops the idea of the past, the stories being told aren't particularly original, which is expected since Cowboy Bebop deliberately pulls from a variety of Western sources. What distinguishes Cowboy Bebop is the level of its direction in telling each of these stories. Musical goddess Yoko Kanno's music plays a large part in elevating scenes to greatness, lending vividness to even the simplest of emotions and providing the energy to the action sequences. In many of the episodes, Yoko Kanno's music paired with beautifully composed long shots make for timeless, classic scenes.

In the first episode, there's the scene with the bloody space ship trialing across the sky. In the fifth episode, there's perhaps one of the greatest sequences in animation of all time, incorporating the song "Rain" followed by "Green Bird". Is it trying to convey some deep philosophical idea? I don't think so. Do you feel intimately what Spike is feeling without him saying a word about his past? Yes. In the sixth episode, there's the scene where the kid plays the harmonica. The simplicity of the scene leaves space for the viewer's own thoughts and emotions to fill in the atmosphere, guided by "Spokey Dokey" and "Digging my Potato". Is it saying anything deep? I don't know. Does it make for an engaging and immersive viewing experience? Yes. The same thing goes for episode 12, where a saxophonist plays "Space Lion" as we see Faye drinking away her troubles at the bar. In episode 10, the lazy, nostalgic coast port setting combined with "Waltz for Zizi" is a story all by itself. So many good moments that are powerful in their understated nature.

The problem is that these moments are often embedded in good, but not great stories. Many of the episodes, like "Stray Dog Strut", "Gateway Shuffle", "Toys in the Attic", "Heavy Metal Queen", "Bohemian Rhasphody", "Mushroom Samba", and "Wild Horses" are simply good, not particularly loads of fun or memorable. Perhaps in a longer series with dramatic, long arcs these episodes would be more enjoyable as relaxed reprieves from the tension, but since Cowboy Bebop is episodic with low stakes most of the time, these episodes stand out as unexceptional. Some viewers might just be enthralled by the novelty of the idea behind the episodes themselves (funny dog chase with Kareem Abdul Jabbar, Alien tribute, trans character, blaxploitation tribute, old space shuttle tribute) but for me the execution of these ideas has to be on point as well, and Cowboy Bebop merely channels the things it's tributing but doesn't pack the umph with them. I didn't laugh at "Stray Dog Strut", the villain in "Gateway Shuttle" was bland (since I've seen it too many times before), "Toys in the Attic"'s suspense was alright but could be better, the character in "Heavy Metal Queen" was alright, the blaxplointation characters lacked character in "Mushroom Samba", and "Wild Horses" was simply a slow, relaxing episode. It's not that the barebones framework in these concepts that are bad, but the magic of the influences being drawn from just isn't there.

Besides these episodes, there were several others that were held back because things weren't explained or they weren't explained well enough, perhaps due to the single episode limitation. In "Sympathy for the Devil", it starts out with good mystery, but things are explained way too fast in a load of exposition and the villain has to monologue before Spike for seemingly no reason. Later, he tries to explain his emotions, but it falls flat because the episode is too rushed. This one would've been great as a two-part episode. I understand that the episode is significant for its message that being stuck in the past is like being stuck in time, but I never got the sense as to why it sucks for the villain. In "Ganymede Elegy", Jet's decision towards the end to resolve his emotional conflict didn't make much sense, or perhaps I'm just not getting something. In "Black Dog Serenade" the final showdown between Jet and his rival is underwhelming and the episode ends before we get Jet's reaction to everything that's transpired. According to one interpretation, though, Jet’s reaction is that he tries to move on from his past. In "Speak like a Child" Faye does have an emotional reaction but we never get to see how the revelations change her. It only serves to deepen her tragedy, but personally I wanted her to also realize that she had the potential to become the good person she was before.

In general, although it's better to be understated than to tell too much, I couldn't discern much character development, as you can scramble the episodes and not notice a difference in the characters themselves and their interactions with the other members of the crew. I'm open to opposing thoughts on this, but I don't think it's a coincidence that this problem shows up in Samurai Champloo as well. The only thing that changes is that the characters become slightly more tolerant towards each other. Even so, any bonds they share are quickly torn when they each are so ready to abandon the crew.

SPOILERS
Spike straight up disregards his partnership with Jet and leaves on at least two different occasions for selfish reasons. I get that he's the lone wolf, stubborn type, but still, he won't budge an inch for anyone else when his own interests conflict with the crew's. When Faye tells him not to leave, he doesn't give a damn and walks off. Speaking of Faye, there's no reason shown for why she decides to join up with the crew again after she runs away. Implicitly, it's because of what Gren said to her, but she also doesn't apologize for leaving the crew out to dry. In addition, Jet was only looking for the money she stole and picked her up by coincidence. I get that Cowboy Bebop's style is very macho and people don't talk about their feelings aloud, preferring to leave things unsaid, but at a certain point you need to give me something to hold onto. OK, even if we accept that resolution, later Faye doesn't waste a second high tailing the hell out of the Bebop when she thinks she's found her family, only to come crawling back because the crew is all she has left. I can understand that Faye is selfish and she would leave a second time, but it's the way she left--- she didn't even look back. That doesn't sound like a strong relationship to me. She's just desperate and looking for a home. The same thing goes with Ed. She just gets up and leaves with Ein one day without even bothering to inform the crew face to face. Just like in Samurai Champloo, Watanabe doesn't seem to want his characters to get too close to one another and it baffles me.
ENDSPOILERS

It's a shame, because many of the episodes that I'm lukewarm towards could be substantially improved with great character banter and having the events of the episode actually change the characters, even incrementally. Instead, most of the time the characters work on separate jobs, rarely working together as a team. They talk to each other like coworkers, not as friends, which is fine in the beginning, but not when you're 20 episodes in. It's not really that big of a problem since Watanabe is so good at crafting interesting, charismatic, and yet deeply human characters that feel alive on the screen. The characters are well designed enough that they can get through 26 episodes without much change or bonding, but the show could've been so much better if they didn't just do that. Much of the time Spike does the field work, Faye's doing something else, Jet's the intel guy, and Ed is off doing her own hacking stuff.

It gets much worse if you watch the dub of Lupin III and realize that Cowboy Bebop's cast is just a pale version of Lupin III's, and the excuse that the characters are stiff with one another because they're not the type to change doesn't fly anymore. Selfish femme fatale with commitment issues? Fujiko pulls that off with much more charisma, sex appeal, and chemistry with men. Straightlaced, stable beard guy? Jigen's the man. Spike doesn't really have a direct analog, as he's nowhere near as silly as Lupin, but the fact remains that the collective chemistry in Lupin III far surpasses that of Cowboy Bebop. Don't believe me? You can just look up the "best of Lupin III dub" on Youtube.

Yeah, it's a nostalgic show about broken people wandering aimlessly in space, but it seems content in reveling in the cool factor of this state rather than exploring the ways their bonds with each other change them (it does, but it's rather weak, as I mentioned before) or how they try to amend that brokenness. When the ending comes, it feels like Cowboy Bebop just decided to end after a series of fun adventures, rather than as a culmination of events that incrementally lead up to the choices that the characters make to bring about that end. You can say that the characters and ideas are developed around the concept of the past, which is valid, but it seems to me that all of it amounts to, “You can’t forget the past, so you’re going to have to carry that weight.” I don’t see how that’s insightful, as obviously your past is going to weigh on you and it’s impossible to completely forget it and move on. The main problem is that simply talking about the past in general is superficial. People's pasts are different and what matters is exactly what about their unique past the keeps them from moving forward? The question for me is, how do we reconcile with our pasts without letting it drag us down? The answer to this question is unique to each individual and their personal struggles, and it isn't as simple as, "just move on". With Cowboy Bebop, I was looking for the crew to find new purpose, form a new family, and do something new with their lives. Jet seems to be the only character who has closure on his past because he engages with the reasons for how things turned out the way they did.

SPOILERS
Of course, Spike breaks that family in an attempt to confront his past, which made no sense to me. Spike may be the coolest animated character ever, and effortlessly so, but his edgy death wish attitude is not cool. "I'm not going to die. I'm going to find out if I'm really alive." *Proceeds to go off to die*. It's alright for a character to have this attitude, as Spike has good reason to. The crux of the problem is that I don’t understand how going back and killing a bunch of mobsters and dying because of it provides any type of emotional catharsis. That’s not confronting your past. Confronting your past would be correcting the mistake you made by not repeating it. You can argue Vicious’s rise of power was Spike’s fault and that Spike is going back to prevent Vicious from taking any more lives, but Spike seems to kill Vicious as a symbolic gesture. Dude, you just repeated the same self destructive, violent behavior that messed up your past in the first place. Perhaps that’s the point, that Spike couldn’t move on, but that’s not confronting the past and that's not saying anything more substantial either.

ENDSPOILERS

The other half of the problem is that your immersion in Cowboy Bebop is highly dependent on whether or not you resonate with its nostalgic longing for the past. I don't. I always look towards the future. If you gave me the chance to go back to my childhood or high school, I wouldn't take it because I'm always trying to build on what came before, so that every day I'm a better person than I was before. Thus, when I see the crew of Cowboy Bebop with all the freedom in the world and long lives ahead of them, I don't understand their unwillingness to look at new possibilities. This wouldn't be a problem if the show did a better job of making me understand why exactly they have trouble letting go instead of talking about the past in general. Spike has trouble letting go of two things: 1) Julia 2) his time in a crime syndicate. It's easy for anyone to understand how hard it is to move on from a loved one, but still, the relationship between Julia and Spike is barely shown and when Julia does show up, she has a pretty boring personality. As for the gang activity, is it that Spike has trouble leaving behind his gangster ways? Does he miss the brotherhood of the gang? Does he need the adrenaline? It's never entirely clear, so it's harder for me to comprehend why this good-looking, talented 23 year old with no laws governing him doesn't go and enjoy his life. It's the same story with Faye's past. If I got a look into her touching relationships with her family and friends, it would be much easier for me to understand why she wants to go back to that so badly because I too, would be feeling her attachment. Whereas in her current situation, I see that Faye is lucky enough to find three other people that give her a place to stay, a job to work, and company, which is a damn good deal for a person who just woke up with nothing left. If the show did go down this more specific role, it would also allow a scene where Spike or Jet inadvertently do a specific gesture for Faye that her family once did, cementing the ragtag family dynamic in a much stronger way that also helps Faye cope with her past in a specific manner. Jet, as I mentioned, gets the best treatment in that I readily understand his pain from his girlfriend leaving him and he gets to throw away his broken watch. At the same time, just like with Spike, the discussion around his attachment to his police days is muddy. Jet is deadset in his methods, straightlaced, and carries the image of a dog who never lets go. This is the mentality that gets him shot and betrayed, which he finds out later. Ok, but now you're retired and free to do whatever you want? In what way are you being tied down by your past? Is it that your straightlaced attitude and stubborness still leads to problems in your life? Well you at least dealt with your commanding nature in Waltz for Zizi already. You find out your partner betrayed you. It's never shown what kind of impact this has on Jet's straightlaced dog persona. Having a past that defines and weighs on a character is basic character writing. The rest of the work has to deal with the specifics of each past instead of settling for a useless general statement of "the character can't move on from the past" or "the character decided to move on".

The Bourne series does this past thing a whole lot better in terms of having a past that you can't escape, because it literally has the CIA constantly try to kill Bourne despite his attempts to start a new life. Mind you, Bourne has no memories just like Faye, and he's been a gun toting martial artist just like Spike. You see Bourne specifically struggle with resisting the programming the CIA gave him and struggle to decide whether or not to kill someone. The Nobel prize winning book Disgrace follows an aged professor who struggles to let go of the days of white privilege and sexual dominance. I'm no rapist, but the book does a good enough job making me at least understand why its main character is and also why he refuses to change. It's not just about not letting go of the past; it's specifically about how to deal with a fall from power and subsequent disgrace. The book doesn't simply say "move on", it follows the protagonist on a long, harrowing journey to starting a new chapter in his life. On JJ Redick's podcast recently, former NBA MVP Steve Nash talked about how hard it was to accept that his days in the NBA were over despite his desperate attempts to keep himself in the game. He then makes peace with it by acknowledging that he has had a good career, and that he can transfer his competitive spirit into other areas in life. For him, letting go of the past was letting go of the spirit of competition and the support from the fans. Satoshi Kon's short film Magnetic Rose deals with an opera singer stuck in the past of her glory days. We clearly understand that she misses performing and the fame that comes with it and that's why she can't let go. The answer to the question of the past is always "you have to make your peace with it and move on". That's kind of a non-statement. The duty of shows that choose to tackle the topic is to explore how exactly this is done on an individual level (and not simply have a character decide to move on abruptly like Ed). Or if people keep falling into a cycle, still explore attempts to move on rather than just drifting around and then pinpoint the factors that counter these attempts to move on. Because despite what the show suggests, the past is not always a weight. It is certainly not for me at this point. A show can take the opposite approach and talk about the past lovingly as fond memories, like Kaiba.

All in all, why am I even bothering to write so much about this issue? If I hadn't known any better, I would've just said that Cowboy Bebop is a good to great action adventure with a lot of style and a sprinkling of brokenness and themes about the past to add some dimension the characters carrying out those fun action adventures. There's a simple message that's repeated for 26 episodes. The thing is, Samurai Champloo is pretty much a mirror image of Cowboy Bebop and you don't see people proclaiming the same things about Samurai Champloo as they do about Cowboy Bebop. Champloo literally has characters doing suicidal things to find out they're really alive TWICE in the same episode. At that point I decided that Watanabe really doesn't have anything more to say beyond "people can't let go of their past so they're stuck in a state of limbo" and my feelings about Cowboy Bebop weren't a fluke. And just like Cowboy Bebop, characters will go through events and things from their past and it doesn't change them at all. Sure, it fleshes them out, but you don't even get to hear their thoughts on it. Later in Samurai Champloo, another character must let go her past by letting of of her hope that her son is alive, but this one moment is more powerful to me than Spike's and Faye's stories.

There's a case to be made that Bebop is really about simply showing how people repeat themselves and can't move on from their old habits, and that's fine but I don't know, maybe I'm just not old enough to connect emotionally with it.

It looks like I'm nitpicking Cowboy Bebop, but in an episodic series, you really have to nail every single minute, and as for its substance, it failed to arouse emotion or resonate with me. I think its massive hype also affected my opinion, as I might have rated it higher if I just went in not knowing anything. Is it a masterpiece? Yes, if you buy into its message about the past. I don’t, but perhaps that will change as I get older. Still, it’s not just a legendary anime--- it’s a legendary work of storytelling in general.

Cowboy Bebop is an episodic anime. Each new episodes deal with a new story, with the exception of the character driven episodes scattered in between, that sometimes span over several episodes. The actual plotline is solid and engaging, leaving you hungering for more answers every time you learn a little more about one of the characters. It is the life that these characters have lead up until the point where the viewer meets them, that really help flesh them out and stop them from becoming one-dimensional stereotypes.

The artstyle in Cowboy Bebop is detailed, creative and fitting. Mostly dull metal and earth tones are mixed with neon colours and really help drive home the feeling of both the loneliness and grandeur that can be experienced in a galactic spanning civilization. One episode you might find yourself in the dusty streets of a Martian colony, the next in a colourful floating casino located in orbit around a planet.

The soundtrack is one of the many high points of Cowboy Bebop. Everything from tribalistic drumbeats to snazzy jazz tunes and even modern electronic music are to be found within the series and all of them help set the mood perfectly. In an anime that is as broad as Cowboy Bebop, a good musical score is immensely helpful in setting each episode apart from another and Yoko Kanno has done a fantastic job.

Now I will touch upon the characters of Cowboy Bebop, who in my opinion are what ultimately manages to carry this show into near perfection. The calm and collected Spike Spiegel, the grumpy and life-hardened Jet Black, the seductive and devious Faye Valentine, along with resident nutball "Ed" and her hyper-intelligent laboratory dog "Ein" make up the main cast of the show. Still very much entertaining, but not really adding anything to the characters or the story and while they are very, very few, they are there.

That being said, Cowboy Bebop is rock-solid from start to finish and raises the bar in terms of soundtrack and characters, earning it a fantastic 10/10.

Cowboy Bebop was a watershed for me . I was 16 years old when I first watched it and I remember how it was a great feeling to watch your episodes and it was sad to watch the last two .
It is very hard to find animes with such great quality nowadays, whether in history or even in art . I watch animes like Shingeki no Kyojin in and I think, " What kind of crap is this ? " . Besides the terrible stories , animations are bad and lazy.
I had never seen an anime with so much adrenaline and with a dose of perfectionism as big as this anime.
Influenced by an amount of things, like jazz movement in the early 40's (?), to noir cinema, Nouvelle Vague, philosophy and even religious themes and samurai laws, Cowboy Bebop's animations flow naturally . You do not feel like everything is mechanized or robotic , even when scenes are repeated numerous times to save the work of animators, wich is hard to see in an anime with so much to say and so little time to show.
And speaking of work, I had never seen a soundtrack as flawless as Cowboy Bebop. A jazz music with influences from rock, pop, classical music and even bossa nova or gypsy music.
The story is about a group of bounty hunters , as you may already know and is told in "closed episodes", which is not very attractive . It was not, it is not and it never will be.
However , the trump card of the story are it's characters. They are strong and I do not speak of their physical size, but their personalities. None of the characters in Cowboy Bebop are typical anime cliches (like Goku or Luff) , nor are predictable and lacking charisma ( like the characters in Shingeki no Kyojin ), they are striking, original and unpredictable. You never know if Spike will lift your opponent or take shoot him in the middle of his forehead .
It is no exaggeration to say that Cowboy Bebop is one of the most influential anime of all time, next to Akira, the films of Hayao Miyazaki and, why not , Akira Toriyama ?
Cowboy Bebop opened Western's eyes to action animes, aimed at an older audience, who were satisfied with comics like Sandman, Allan Moore's comics and among others, and influenced all sectors of our artistic side of the world, since cinema, to comics, music and even literature.
You can find influences from Cowboy Bebop in mainstream films such as Tarantino and Matrix, to underground artists like Dawn of Midi and even King Krule .
Sure Cowboy Bebop was also very influenced ( Lupin III and the movement of jazz are just some of the more obvious influences), but it was the mastery in uniting all these references (musical, artistic, visual, literary, phylosophical and religious) that make this anime the best anime of all times , in the humble opinion of this fan .
