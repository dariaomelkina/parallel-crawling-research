It's a show that was fantastic, maybe even a perfect storm, for its time.

It had good art and animation. It had a blend of episodic content and a central plot. It had a fairly consistent presentation that took itself seriously, with minimal shifts to off-model or differently-styled art. Amazing, distinct soundtrack. An iconic English dub that was one of the first that could make people seriously consider that dubbing quality could match the original Japanese.

But I think in many ways, modern anime has caught up to or even surpassed Bebop. Many shows get good dubs. Many shows have good OSTs. Many shows have very expressive artwork that sticks to a particular mood. There are shows with better episodic content and there are "background plots" that are told better or more-interwoven throughout the length of the series.

It's not that Bebop aged poorly, but it was just somewhat ahead of its time. If it released today, people would likely complain that most of the episodic storylines were bad, goofy, or forgettable. Let's face it, whenever anyone praises Bebop, it's for the main story episodes, mushroom samba, the fridge episode, and maybe Faye's backstory episode, and that's it. That's less than half of the 24 episodes. People would balk that Faye's sexpot character design is pointless fanservice. Same goes for the bounty hunter TV show where one of the cohosts is a girl not wearing a shirt. People should hate Ed, because Ed is annoying.

THIS REVIEW CONTAINS MANY SPOILERS

Watching Cowboy Bebop feels like drinking a nice cold beer after a hard day at work. You can just sit back and relax while watching octane-powered chases and action scenes along with great jazz music.

The story goes like this: we follow the misadventures of Spike Spiegel and Jet Black, broke bounty hunters aboard the Bebop in a futuristic sci-fi context. There is no complex plot to wrap your head around -- we naturally and effortlessly learn more about the past of our beloved main characters along the anime as they partner up with the amnesiac femme fatale Faye Valentine, orphan whiz kid Radical Edward and the genetically-modified dog Ein.

Spike is our action man, a non-womanizing version of James Bond. His spontaneity and dry humor more than make up for his lack of flirting, though. A prolific thief with unparalleled agility, his weapons of choice are pistols and martial arts. Thankfully, his agility is no carte blanche for every challenge of his, as he often gets shot and hurt along the anime... his unpretentious walk always gets me. Contrasting with Spike, Jet is a behind the scenes techie who acts as support, identifies their next targets and makes sure every runs smoothly. Jet has a friend in every corner of galaxy, which makes viewers wonder about his past as the episodes advance.

Interestingly, the crew of the Bebop present a truly chaotic sense of justice, in the sense of D&D. Sometimes, they do not seem to care about, or even understand, the impact of their actions. Consequently, they downplay their role in the fate of humanity in galaxy.

Other times, their personal lives conflict with their pursuit of money, which leads to nice ethical dilemmas. This is the means through which we learn more about our beloved misfits. Since I chose not to care about how much I am spoiling here, why not reveal all my thoughts? The relation with the past is a major theme in Cowboy Bebop. The decisions and personal lives of the crew of the Bebop reveal different ways to deal with the past: we may run from it, we may chase it, we may be forced to face it or perhaps we may want to chase it -- yet it might not explain the present anymore!

The icing on the cake is the ambiance of Cowboy Bebop. There is zero political correctness -- cigarettes and drug trafficking are all over the place, exactly as they were in wild west movies. Gory scenes are always to be expected... and even ones which hint at sex. Maybe I am just too used to western-style clean movies, yet it is just refreshing to see an anime which does not try to hide it all. At first, it seemed to me that the sexual tension with Faye was underplayed. On a second moment, I realize it was better like this: the "born sexy yesterday" trope is already present on Faye Valentine -- an emphasis on sexual tension with her would inevitably lead to boob jokes or male-centered fan-service. (so kudos to the producers for not having fallen in such pitfall)

For a sci-fi anime, the incoherent mix of technologies used by the characters would feel contradictory -- maybe even nonsensical -- if noticed anywhere else: pistols and machine-guns are the weapons of choice, while we see more futuristic guns in spaceships and occasional missile launchers. If there is anything sacred here, it is its aesthetics: this anachronic mix is dosed gracefully as to the cultural significance certain guns are endowed with. This seemingly incoherent mix... it just works. As for the revolvers, this is an anime about (space) cowboys, after all...

The way I look at it, Cowboy Bebop is a must-see for any otaku young and old, period. Why restrict it to otakus? It simply is a must-see. It contains iconic scenes of anime as a genre, pays homage to several pop-culture icons and references classical scenes of movies.

To finish, Cowboy Bebop has earned its legendary status without relying on a 2deep4u plot crutch justification. To be fair, Cowboy Bebop is not just a nice cold beer. It is a fine whisky.
