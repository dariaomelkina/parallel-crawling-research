The story is set in a space western setting - Spike and Jet, who own a ship called the Bebop. They travel the Solar system as bounty hunters, chasing wanted criminals to earn money. Along the way, they also pick up two women; the debt-laden Faye Valentine and the playful kid and computer genius Edward. Also a genius dog named ein.
Anway I am not a huge fan of the mosnter of the week formula, I prefer a stories to follow certain arcs, however not every episode in this story is a monster of the week. Those episodes are always good regardless, the reason why I'm giving cowboy bebop an 8 is because its inconsistent. Some episodes like the last two episodes are ten out of ten, some are 7 outs of ten. the episodes focusing on the past of the main characters are good and a few individuals one-off episodes, but for me it isn't consistent enough to earn a ten.
Now let us talk about the characters
Bebop has a theme about the past and how you can't run away from it. All the characters in the ENTIRE SHOW are effected by the past. This includes the bounties they chase , who are running from the crimes they committed whether they did it for good reasons or bad.

Spike: Most obvious. He's running from his time with Vicious and the Red Dragons. He had to fake his death to escape his past, and his arc ends "positively" with him accepting his past and accepting death. I felt like I was watching a dream i never wake up from, before i knew it the dream was all over" is a telling line of his. Yeah, basically one of the themes of the show, that he even talks about with Faye, is that it's useless to hold onto the past. You should let it go and live in the moment, freely.

Of course, he never lived by this advice himself. He's shown to constantly be held by the past and an inability to let go of Julia. He consistently takes off from the Bebop to go drink and think about things and lives in a state of lethargy because of his perceived loss. He finds Julia at the end of the series and when she's killed, he decides to spare the Bebop crew anymore attacks from the Red Dragon by confronting Vicious directly... He also does this to avenge Julia and bring an end to the cycle of living in the past and present at once.

Faye: Second most obvious. Her past is completely erased from her memory and she spends most of her time on Bebop trying to suppress her sadness with it by going to dog/horse races and drinking. When the VHS tape comes to her near the end of the show, she starts remembering more and more, until she finds her family home has been torn down and she has no memory of it outside of the video tape. Her arc ends negatively, accepting that she'll never have those memories back.

Jet: Least obvious. Jets glory days are behind him, and he left behind a successful job as a police officer (after being set up for a fall by his partner) and the love of his life who left him because she felt too dependent on him. He never had the family he wanted so he tries very hard to make a family out of the Bebop crew. He loves Ed, thinks of Spike as his brother, he is the only member outside of Ed that interacts regularly with Ein, and at least tolerates Faye. Jet's arc is a lot less dynamic than Spike and Faye, probably because he's supposed to be the moral center that drives Spike's story, but he's an old man past his prime.
Ed is a lot less dynamic that Spike and Faye for obvious reasons, but Ed's father was never very attentive to Ed and left Ed in an orphanage because he couldn't keep them around while working his intense job as an Earth cartographer. The Bebop crew, Jet excluded, doesn't care for Ed and disregards them often.

Ed's past is about rejection, and Ed seems (I say seems because Ed never speaks clearly about themselves in the show lol) to come to the acceptance that they like being alone, and the family they made on Bebop isn't what they need. Ein comes along of course because they are best friends (And because Watanabe needed to jettison Ed and Ein before the climax) but Ed finds solace in being alone. Ed's arc ends in their last episode when they write Goodbye across the bow and venture across Earth finding new weird shit to do.
Vicious: It sounded like Vicious wanted Spike to come back to the syndicate, perhaps be partners once more during their confrontation at the church.

Just before spike was pushed out the stained glass window, Vicious was furious when spike told him that he put past behind him.

Perhaps Vicious was planning on having spike be a part of syndicate once he succeeded with the coup. Then again he did kill Mao. Everyone was waiting for Spike to return, perhaps Vicious wasn't an exemption.

The whole scene is great from start to finish. It's the first time they've seen each other in years, since Spike left and had his near brush with death. We can only speculate what their relationship was like before everything but with the brief flashbacks during Spike's fall they appeared to be very close friends. They most likely had very similar childhoods (lack of parents being around/never there to begin with) poor struggling to survive on the streets of Mars. Then Mao comes along and sees their potential in the syndicate and takes them under his wing, becoming the closest thing they've had to a father/family. From then until Spike leaves they were probably constantly working together(definitely some friendly rivalry trying to one up each other) becoming like brothers, the first time I watched that scene when Vicious talks about them sharing the same blood I thought they were actually brothers. Now I realize he meant it symbolically. I think Vicious feels betrayed by Spike and I agree I think Vicious wanted him to come back and be his number 2. Then again Vicious may have wanted to just kill him to eliminate the threat. Little is known about Spike's past and even less of Vicious's so its hard to say...

Cowboy Bebop is one of the best shows I have watched, period. The first episode sets the tone, with a seemingly upbeat and almost flippant protagonist, who reveals himself to be cunning, battle-worn, and melancholic. This start sets up a string of subtly interconnected stories that begin to unravel the past as we come to understand what type of show we are actually watching. The true marvel of this show is what we are not shown. Cowboy Bebop takes place after the climax, after the heroics, after the action; it gives us characters that have taken the punches, lost parts of themselves along the way, and are seeking to cling on to a sense of meaning in their post-story lives. It is fitting that the main characters are basically drifting through nothing, as this is true to who they have become.

This brings us to the introduction of Faye Valentine and Ed; the two characters representing hope and the future. Faye brings mischief to the group, a sense of change to shake up their lives and wake Spike and Jet out of their melancholic stupor. Her developing feelings for Jet give hope that he may awaken to what he has before him, and bring him from his prison of the past. Ed serves as a beacon of joy, laughter, and new life for these middle aged veterans, and brings some of the best story moments of the series.

The music of Cowboy Bebop is legendary, and acts as a focal point of the series, bringing life to each scene and truly resonating long after the show has gone. And there are many powerful moments the show presents, touching on moral issues, healing wounds of the past, and giving closure to chapters of life. The ending itself was beautiful, tragic, and captivating; and I think it captures the soul of the series vividly. I highly recommend this to anyone willing to give it a chance

Well, if this isn't the poster child for "a pure and true anime classic" I don't know what would be. Cowboy Bebop is visually dated but is otherwise timeless and it has rightfully gone down, and will continue to go down, as one of the greatest anime series ever.

Art-

The art was perfectly fine, if not quite good, for it's time and it is now, understandably, a bit dated. Reviewing this anime in the present as opposed to objectively for when it was created, the art is likely the weakest point of the show, but that's largely because everything else is exceptionally good and time has been kind to the other aspects of the show.

Sound-

Cowboy Bebop has one of the most acclaimed soundtracks ever. The opening sequence is among the greatest of all time, the jazz throughout the series is exciting and of high quality and the perfect song always seems to play during any given action sequence. I don't see how any objective person, regardless of personal taste, could give the sound of Cowboy Bebop anything other than a 10.

The voice acting is solid and there are no low points, but the outstanding soundtrack steals the show in the sound department.

Character-

The characters and the story are likely the biggest factor as to what makes this anime truly special. The crew is super memorable, the lead is one of the most iconic anime badasses of all time and the sheer variety of villains, planets, scenarios and mini-stories, which eventually build up to an exceptionally good ending, combine to make one of the overall most unique and memorable experiences in the history of the medium.

This anime truly has a little bit of everything and it just works. Other shows have tried to replicate this and they almost always end up a convoluted mess but Cowboy Bebop pulls this off masterfully in a way no other show, prior or since, has been able to achieve.

Enjoyment-

There isn't a single episode without action, there's a plethora of gunfights and fist fights and you genuinely give a damn about the characters and the trouble they're getting into, so it's damn near impossible not to enjoy this show. There are so many stories contained within the overall larger narrative and many of them have feel good endings that simply make you happy to watch. Perhaps how enjoyable the show is to watch, even all this time later, is the biggest reason it has aged so well and is still so revered today.

Overall-

A timeless classic and a masterpiece, Cowboy Bebop, while old and visually dated by today's standards, is one of the greatest anime series of all time.

+Pulls off the "a little bit of everything" formula perfectly
+Amazing music
+You'll genuinely care about the characters and what happens to them
+A 26 episode show that honestly truly has no bad episodes. It just doesn't

-Visually dated

OBJECTIVE RATING- 10
PERSONAL ENJOYMENT RATING- 9

Cowboy Bebop is the least anime-like anime I've ever seen, aside from its animation, almost nothing in it feels like an anime. It feels more like a very very good american-made movie just in the form of an anime.

But yet at the same time, if it hadn't been an anime, it wouldn't have been anywhere as good as it is. Its art style and animation still look gorgeous even today, and they truly bring the world to life.

And that is one of Bebop's biggest strengths. Its atmosphere is presented beautifully, it just instantly immerses you into its world so flawlessly, and makes you feel apart of it. This is further dome with its soundtrack.

Im not too into Jazz, but Bebop's soundtrack is probably my favourite soundtrack in anime next to FLCL, its variety of jazz, and sometimes even blues/rock music brings the tone and atmosphere to live even further, and every single scene in the show the music manages to nail this out of the park.

Bebop is also episodic, meaning a different story every episode with a few here and there progressing the overall story. I wasnt a fan of this initially, but bebop does it so well i enjoyed it, and it gave the episodes a nice diversity, ranging from funny, to intense, to slow and emotional to haunting and chilling. It also manages to wrap it all up in a conclusion that is heartbreaking, but one that couldn't be more perfect.

But the real heart of bebop is its characters. Each of the main four characters is deep in their own right and the show wouldn't work without a single one of them, thanks to their highly entertaining dynamic.

The thing is about these characters, is that they aren't characters that start off a certain way and eventually grow and develop as the series go on. Instead they're characters who already have grown and developed, but their past that made them that way is slowly revealed over time, till they each eventually reach their beautiful conclusions.

This strong set of characters is aided by the spot-on English cast, who each bring their character to life to the fullest extent. The English Dub for Cowboy Bebop is often considered one of the best anime dubs of all time, and it's hard to disagree. While the original Japanese version is certainly passable, the dub feels far more natural and communicates the emotions much more effectively, and I feel is the definitive version to watch this series in, especially with Steven Blum as Spike Spiegel being one of the best voice acting performances I've heard in anything, let alone an anime.

Immersive art style, mesmerizing atmosphere, perfect soundtrack, and tragic and deep characters, Cowboy Bebop is an excellent classic anime series thats worth a watch at least once. The only flaws i had were that there were some episodes which I really didn't like, and this isn't exactly a flaw but it wasn't amazingly mind-blowing or life changing like my top 5 anime, so it's not enough for me to give it a 10. But still I enjoyed it immensely and would consider one of my favorites. And I'm giving this one a decent to strong 9/10

Cowboy bebop is a magical experience that you don't fully appreciate until it's over. The nature of the show is odd but is executed so perfectly, the many adventures and characters we see and all thoughtfully and carefully put into the story to address something related to the main cast. Saying I love this show is an understatement, it was the show that got me into anime; I still chase that high I got from it the very first time watching it. Well written, well thought out, and perfectly executed is what this show is. It's seems like a love letter to me personally, this is because it discusses some of my favorite themes, the older I get the more I understand what it means and the more I appreciate it's colorful and authentic explorations and tragedies. A wild mix of genres and settings with insane adaptability makes this one of my favorite shows of all time.

The storys is shrouded by mystery it leaves you in the dark, give you very little information and when it all ends it leaves you with wanting more. However, as interesting and complex the main story may be the real star when it comes to the story telling is the episodic nature of the show and how in every episode we get a new and unique story that fills you with an odd sense of dread, re-watching the show many of these stories are reflections of the main cast and how they live with their past. Every episode is meaningful and takes you on a journey in a new part of the world. The world building is also fantastic, it's set in this dystopian space society in the future where earth is beyond repair and just a junkyard, the world reflects the atmosphere of the planets they visit very well, for example, Earth feels like home, a place to contemplate, and a place where past and present is linked. In contrast Venus is the sister planet to earth, it's being rebuilt and taken care off environmentally which seems tragic and ironic but that describes episode 8, the episode set in Venus, perfectly. Ironic and Tragic.

The art is fantastic, the first thing I noticed is that it's very visually appealing if you need an example watch up to episode 5. The colors used always reflect the tone of the show perfectly, the art style makes the goofiness of the show apparent, and the design of personal belongings reflect their characters very well. My favorite example of the use of color and tone in the show is episode 6, at the very end with 'Wen' and how the red colored background contrasting the black soil that seems decomposed and used works so perfectly with the harmonica being played adds so much impact to what happened. In addition the shows artistic style also helps them portray powerful foreshadowing and symbolism, with the use of objects, colors, and stories pay attention to episode 17 and you'll understand what I mean.

Sound in this show is the driving theme behind everything, take one listen to the Cowboy Bebop OST and you'll be hooked and engaged with 20 different genres of music and tracks so good it will make you want to watch this show all over again, this time paying attention to the placement of these songs and their significance. From the Starting theme of 'Tank!' to the amazing blues and jazz we hear throughout the show, music is the constant in a show that changes every single episode. Even the titles of each episode are based off a classic song. Many of the songs in the show are jazzy or blues heavy songs that reflects the tragic trend of the show, and every single song is intentionally and carefully placed to set a mood or tone. I don't know where to start nitpicking these songs and their meanings to the stories but I recommend watching episode 11 and the final two episodes of Bebop. I think the silence in episode 11 is excellent because it sets the episodes tone as calming, and the final two episodes use the blues to set a tragic and depressing tone.

The character of bebop whether it's the villain, a character we see once, or the main cast all have thought and detail put into them. They are carefully crafted to look as they act; to be physical embodiment of their stereotypes. The main theme in Cowboy Bebop is the passage of time and how our past affects us. Every single character introduced has a troubled past they are attempting to cover or escape, they deal with the past by indulging in substance abuse, gambling, killing, etc... it shows you how when there's something holding you back you need to cut it before it is the end of you. Emptiness is also a major theme, how time passes by you and now you're a hundred years old with nothing valuable that you remember. However, I believe the more I watch Bebop the more I get out of it, with age i'll find something new that will have significant meaning to my life. Through the diversity of the characters, settings, stories, and the show itself it really allows someone to nitpick details and themes that might influence you at different stages of your life. It's an introspective journey through the galaxy and your mind, and everyone will draw a different meaning from Cowboy Bebop

This show has a good dub, what the fuck world do we live in. How is this show so perfect, watch it for yourself. "The work, which becomes a new genre itself, will be called Cowboy Bebop." Bebop is a masterpiece in setting tone; the music, story, character design, themes, and settings work together so well. If there was one anime I think everyone should watch it's this one.

Please watch it, 10/10 on everything.

[STORY: 7/10]
I'll say that the overall story is good, but what holds it back is its tendency to be cliché and even boring at times. A couple of episodes are completely irrelevant to the main story, and with only twenty-six episodes and a movie to portray this story, it can get annoying, as those slots could've been used to better develop the characters or feature a way more interesting adventure.

[ART: 10/10]
The art for this show is older and very pleasing to the eye. Alot of the character design and set pieces are very unique and creative, putting together an atmosphere that is epic in scope and beautiful to look at.

[SOUND: 10/10]
The show is known for its iconic soundtrack containing heavy jazz influence and it really sets the style and mood for the show. The music fits the designated scenes and is very pleasant to listen to. The voice actors are very good and give their characters life, and I've heard the dub is just as good as the sub, which is a rare occurrence.

[CHARACTERS: 6/10]
There are some very interesting (albeit not all very original) characters that occupy the main cast of this show. The main downside is a lack of character development throughout. Relationships are built, but unless it's a major part of the story, mostly nothing changes. Almost each and every episode or set of episodes follows a separate adventure that could be watched in any order and it wouldn't make much difference. This isn't inherently bad, but for the characters we grow to care for, we want to see them grow and change, finding out who they are and why they do what they do. Sadly, we just don't get enough of that here.

[ENJOYMENT/OVERALL: 7/10]

You're a Gen Y or a Gen Z kid but your parent is a Baby Boomer and the only way they are going to watch an anime and at the very least, understand one, is if you now show them Cowboy Bebop. They might hate it. They might love it. But, you did your part in at least, showing them this classic animated series directed by Shinichiro Watanabe. You see, it's not just in Bebop's pop cultural references that they can make connections to from the times they were growing up with Bruce Lee and Bob Dylan, but that this series has a soundtrack that pretty surpasses an entire list of those from American television shows. You can throw in movie soundtracks in this argument as well and still make a good case as to why this show had the greatest one of them all. With genres of jazz, rock, soul, and pop, Kanno's music both synchronizes with the episodes perfectly and acts as a cohesive album independent from the show just when casually listening to it in the car on your way home from work. Her renowned track "Tank!" sets the stage as the series opening and classic Jazz piece. An addicting fusion of Jazz and rock having shades of the old Lupin theme from the 80s. Both openings exercise a hyper-stylization of color as it might indicate a throwback to 70s technicolor and the boom of american cinema when westerns began to die out and the action cop thriller took it's place.

But, all that being said, I'd like to really begin with this review of the show without giving too much away. First, I'll begin with the title "Cowboy Bebop"---it doesn't indicate that what we are about to explore is the Ying and the Yang of culture between the phenomenon of western and jazz. Perhaps the choice in juxtaposition is random, hinting at an indication of something larger and more important from the past in the late 20th century when Hollywood was coming out with tons of genres in entertainment. In Bebop's galaxy where bounty hunters survive on very little and when their interactions with people reveal events from the present and past, we have our main characters, Spike, Jet, and Faye question about why they still exist. Here, in this world, celebrity is somewhat forgotten and the once-promising culture from earth was completely destroyed by a meteor. Civilization is now on Mars though, much of it's culture is not very different from the one that was lost before. Fragmentation that is caused by technology affects us more-or-less the same for as long as tradition is continually passed down and reiterated. A VHS tape from the 90s is now nothing more than some priceless artifact stored in some second hand shop in a dark alleyway.

The series best episode "Ballad of Fallen Angels" centers on the backstory of Spike Spiegel and we get a clearer understanding that Bebop is not just a sunrise sci-fi with an over-the-top budget attempting to please it's crowd of Trek nerds. Bebop acts on one level as a love letter to a lot to 80s cyberpunk anime like Goku Midnight Eye and Space Adventure Cobra, the construction of Spike's character alone acts as not only a continuation of those from the past but as a more matured, 3-dimensional version of one. For instance, the eye that Goku gets in Kawjiri's anime was replaced with a fake, cyber eye that gave him control over a lot of scientific devices in the story and helping him defeat the bad guys. Spike has a similar characteristic though, their is a more human, realistic side to the outcome of his operation in how the eye affects him emotionally. The influence of Bob Dylan and Bruce Lee that went into the design of Spike truly separates him apart from these other rugged action heroes. And truthfully, the character Spike is simply a lot better than all of those action heroes in the 80s in general. Because this show is so well animated, it makes us want to learn more about Spike's haunted past and what caused his edge in the first place.

The other protagonists, Jet and Faye are very interesting as well with the construction of Jet being inspired by the song Black Dog by Led Zeppelin, and Faye Valentine inspired by the song "My Funny Valentine." They both seem to work well as supporting characters as they are more easily to crack than Spike is.

It seems pointless to review Cowboy Bebop given that in the past 20 years it's been hailed as most likely the best anime ever. And I certainly don't have much to add to that assessment, but I will add my thoughts to the collective wisdom.

First, the art. I absolutely love the retro 60's Golgo 13 inspired style on the landscape and vehicles. If you grew up watching Japanese cartoons from the 60's and 70's like I did, seeing that style updated in such as cool and futuristic way is just the shit. It really contributes to the sparse, lonely atmosphere of the Bebop universe. Just as Japanese cartoons in the 60's and 70's were sparse and bare out of necessity - it was expensive to hand animate everything so they relied on techniques such as panning the camera over static images - the same aesthetic works here.

The characterizations are top notch. Spike, the not-so-bright but very stylistic ex-gangster trying to eke out a living as a "cowboy" bounty hunter. Always right on the edge of success but somehow living with failure right in front of his face. You underestimate his skills until he puts them on display and you sit and watch in disbelief as he deftly eviscerates his quarry. I'd cry if my eyeballs hadn't already melted out of their sockets.

Jet, the mysterious ex-cop owner of the Bebop. Has a, let's call it "complicated" relationship with Spike and Faye. Hates how much he cares about Spike and relies on his comradeship. Just a guy trying to make a buck in a crazy universe stuck with a bunch of assholes that don't respect him or carry their weight, and he wouldn't have it any other way.

Faye, the femme fatale. Saddled with the soap opera trope of a lost memory, she has become more than the sum of her missing parts. Faye is fiercely independent, completely capable, and the prototype for the modern female lead character. Completely owns every scene she's in and I'd call you a liar if you said you wouldn't watch the shit out of a solo Faye series.

Ed - whatever the hell Ed is - the chaos monkey that knows everything and nothing all at the same time. Where did she get those skills and how does she always manage to tempt fate and synchronize her life completely the opposite of the forces of death to stay alive? We'll never know, unfortunately.

Music: if Cowboy Bebop is legendary for one thing, it's the music. From composition to performance the music is timeless, flawless, and incredible. Do yourself a favor and listen to the soundtrack.

Favorite episodes: the one where Ed feeds the rest of the crew the magic mushrooms to see if they are safe to eat. Hilarious and sad to watch all at the same time, you feel bad for laughing at Spike's Sisyphean walk up the unending stairs - nah, you don't feel bad. Other favorite episode: Faye's tape, Spike and Jet crawl the bowels of the crumbling city to find the ancient electronics museum and bring back a Beta instead of a VHS. Spike's insistence that it would work if Jet just crammed the tape in the slot the right way is every human's struggle with technology ever. And so exemplary of Cowboy Bebop - a story makes us both laugh and cry with the same breath.

Please watch if you are a human being that appreciates having your life and spirit enriched with the twin gods of happiness and sadness.

Wether you have experience watching anime or are just a newbie like me, you have probably heard about the so popular and controversial Cowboy Bebop.

The anime raises two main discussions that i would like to mention in this review:

- That it's overrated

- That it's "style over substance"

To the first statement, i partially disagree. To the second one, i am utterly against. Just let me explain.

I can understand why a person would harbour the opinion that Cowboy Bebop is so popular that people tend to automatically rate it a ten even before starting the series, saying that it has absolutely no flaws whatsoever. No piece of work is free of having flaws, not even this masterpiece of an anime, and that is a fact that we all must face. I can mention a couple if you want me to, like the episodic way in which the series was arranged bringing too many convenient coincidences and abrupt conclusions to some conflicts. But when those flaws acquire little relevance compared to the positive aspects and do not tamper with the overall quality of the show, that's when we find a good series worth watching.

The enjoyment a person experiences while watching it is completely personal depending on his or her own preferences and that is why i can totally understand the different ratings this series gets. But something i consider extremely important when reviewing any piece of work is separating our own subjective opinion from the objective view. Subjectively, you may enjoy the episodic arrangement of the anime and its theme or not, but objectively it is almost impossible to state that this series does not have lots of magnificent effort put to good use wether its in the audiovisuals or the character development or any other technical aspect. The combination of those two is what makes a propper review.

That is why it really bugs me when i find people saying cowboy bebop is overrated and then continue justifying it with facts that revolve more around personal tastes than anything else.

I, for example, don't tend to enjoy nor episodical nor space anime and that is why it took me so long to pick this up. And yes, it was a little slow to me because of that, but the fact that i enjoyed it anyways and even finished it actually amazed is a proof of good quality. I could have rated it a 9, but i realised it wasn't fair for the series itself when comparing it to the other animes in my list and the ratings i gave them, because Cowboy Bebop seriously seems like it's made of something else.

Moving on to the "style over substance" matter, i do think that's truly a complete nonsense. Yes, the style is magnificent and we all can agree to that. The art is good though not a wonder to me, but the animation is flawless, especially in the facial expresions and in the space related movements (meaning laws of physics are not broken and it actually seems realistic and coherent). The music is something from another dimension really. Someone could think that it would be a total mess considering all the different genres of music being played that can either match or not the theme of the anime, but in the end that does not even matter because it mixes so well the scene that it's overwhelming. It's like some words are cut because they are not need, it's the music that speaks to you about the atmosphere and how characters feel.

But style is not merely enough to make an anime good, and here comes the substance of it which i think is fantastic. This is the first anime i have seen so far that made me really think of the characters as real people. They are so well done and their background is for each one of them so very well developed i was really amazed. The action scenes provide their moments of badassery for the characters but are not overly pretentious and do not cross the line of coherence and possibility. The plot could have it's occasional flaws here or there, but it is obvious that it was carefully sewed into place, so as to establish a sequencial plot involved with the past of the characters even in an episodical series.

Conclusion: please watch it. It really stands out how this anime is different from the rest and a unique piece of work.
